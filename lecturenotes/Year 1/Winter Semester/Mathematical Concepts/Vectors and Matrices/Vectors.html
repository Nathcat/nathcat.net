Vectors

Vectors are a way of representing a position in an n-dimensional space, most commonly 2 or 3 dimensions.

They consist of multiple components, the number of components is equal to the number of dimensions the vector exists in, and each component describes a displacement along an axis for that dimension. Hence by combining these displacements, we have a final position.

There are generally two ways in which Vectors are represented, as rows, and as columns:

$\displaystyle{ \vec{a}=\left( \begin{array}{cccc} \\a_1&a_2&\cdots &a_n \end{array} \right) }$ and $\displaystyle{ \vec{b}=\left( \begin{array}{c} b_1\\b_2\\\vdots\\b_n \end{array} \right) }$

It is common to think about vectors as a type of matrix, as they are commonly used in operations with matrices, but we may interpret them differently to a matrix.

Vector Transposition

Vectors may be transposed to transform them between row and column representations.

$ \vec{x}= \left(\begin{array}{c} x_1\\ x_2\\ \vdots\\ x_n \end{array}\right) \in \mathbb{R}^n \to \vec{x}^T=\left(\begin{array}{cccc} \\x_1&x_2&\cdots &x_n \end{array}\right) $

Remember that multiplying a row vector by a column vector gives a real, single dimensional value:

$\displaystyle{ \left( \begin{array}{cccc} \\p_1&p_2&\cdots &p_n \end{array} \right) \left( \begin{array}{c} x_1\\ x_2\\ \vdots\\ x_n \end{array} \right) = \sum_{i=1}^{n}p_ix_i\in\mathbb{R} }$

So, $\vec{x}^T\vec{x}\in\mathbb{R}$

Euclidean Norm

A common application of this is in computing the Euclidean norm, or "length" of a vector.

The dot / scalar product: $\displaystyle{ \vec{x}\cdot\vec{y}=\vec{x}^T\vec{y}=\sum_{i=1}^nx_iy_i }$

And hence the euclidean norm: $\displaystyle{ {\|\vec{x}\|} =\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i^2}=\sqrt{\vec{x}\cdot\vec{x}} }$

Orthogonality

Two vectors are Orthogonal if they are perpendicular to each other, that is, their dot product is equal to 0:

$\displaystyle{ \vec{a}\cdot\vec{b}= \sum_{i=1}^{n}a_ib_i=0 }$
